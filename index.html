Research Projects



When the Storm Breaks Expectations: Reference Dependence and the Demand for Flood Insurance [JMP]

[PAPER]  Households face rare but potentially catastrophic losses from hurricanes and must often rely on imperfect forecasts to guide protective action. This paper links hurricane forecasts to flood insurance uptake, using forecast errors as novel, exogenous reference points to study behavior under risk. The analysis reveals strong asymmetries in response: insurance demand increases sharply after unanticipated storm strikes (false misses), rises modestly when damage is correctly forecast (true hits), and declines following false alarms. These patterns are consistent with reference-dependent preferences and loss aversion, where deviations between expected and realized storm outcomes shift the perceived value of insurance. By applying a behavioral lens to real-world risk decisions, the paper offers new evidence on how expectations shape protective behavior and highlights the importance of salience and misprediction in insurance markets.

The Missing Piece: Experimental Evidence on Salience and Insurance

This paper investigates the impact of salience on insurance demand through a novel laboratory experiment that simulates real-world risk using physical uncertainty. Participants are tasked with replicating LEGO figures using a randomized set of bricks, with a nonzero probability that a required piece is missing. In the control condition, participants receive their bag without explanation, while in the salience treatment, they observe the experimenter physically assembling their bag—making the risk of missing pieces more visually salient. In the second round, all participants are offered insurance guaranteeing a complete set, and willingness to pay is elicited using the incentive-compatible Becker-DeGroot-Marschak (BDM) mechanism. By manipulating salience through direct observation rather than framing, the design offers a novel test of how perceptual salience affects risk perception and valuation. Preliminary results show that the salience treatment significantly increases willingness to pay for insurance, consistent with behavioral theories of attention and decision-making under uncertainty.

Building Effort: Reference Points and Productivity in a LEGO Task

This paper examines how reference points influence effort provision through a real-effort laboratory experiment. In the first round, participants are asked to assemble 15 LEGO figures within 25 minutes and are compensated based on the number of minutes remaining upon completion. In a second round, participants are randomly assigned to one of two treatments. In Treatment 1, the required output is reduced to 10 figures, with the same per-minute reward. In Treatment 2, the original 15-figure target is retained, but participants receive a $0.75 reward per minute saved. By independently manipulating performance expectations and pay, the design identifies whether a downward shift in the performance goal—serving as a lower reference point—leads to reduced effort, even when economic incentives remain unchanged. The experiment offers a novel and transparent test of reference-dependent effort provision, isolating the behavioral effects of goal-setting from standard monetary responses.
(data collection is currently underway)

About Me



I'm an Economics PhD candidate at Michigan State University

I’m a behavioral and experimental economist by day, and a hurricane-data-sleuth and Lego-specalist by… well, also by day. My job market paper explores how reference dependence shapes flood insurance demand through hurricane forecast errors. I love teaching economics—especially when I can bring data to life, spark a good debate, or sneak a behavioral experiment into the classroom. I live with two cats: Nash (yes, named after the economist) and Lulu. I also have more than two houseplants. When I’m not in my office, you can find me basking in the sun, baking something sweet, reading something weird, or nursing a plant back to life.
